Monarch Money Review — The Best Personal Finance App Nobody Talks About

Monarch Money does almost everything Mint used to do — and several things Mint never could — making it the most complete personal finance app available right now.

ProspectAI Editorial
June 2026
7 min read
✓ Affiliate disclosure

When Intuit shut down Mint in January 2024, millions of users were suddenly homeless. The obvious alternatives — YNAB, Copilot, Personal Capital — each solved part of the problem but introduced new ones: too rigid, too Apple-only, too investment-heavy. Monarch Money had been quietly building since 2021, and when the Mint exodus happened, it was ready. The timing looked lucky. The product was not.

Monarch Money is a subscription-based personal finance app that connects your bank accounts, credit cards, investments, and loans into one dashboard, then layers on budgeting, goal tracking, net worth monitoring, and collaborative tools for couples or households. That description sounds like every other finance app. The execution is where Monarch separates itself — and where a few genuine frustrations still live.

This review is based on extended hands-on use of the platform. We tested account syncing across multiple financial institutions, built out a full budget, stress-tested the cash flow reports, and evaluated the AI-powered features that Monarch has been rolling out. Here is what we found.

What Monarch Money Actually Does

At its core, Monarch Money aggregates your financial accounts through a combination of data providers — primarily Plaid and Finicity — and organizes transactions automatically using category rules you can customize. The interface is clean and browser-based, with iOS and Android apps that mirror the desktop experience closely rather than feeling like a stripped-down afterthought.

The main pillars of the product are:

  • Budgeting: A flexible, category-based budget that works on a monthly rollover system. You can set fixed amounts per category or use a "flex" mode that adjusts based on actual spending patterns.
  • Cash flow tracking: Income and expense summaries by month, with drill-down into individual transactions and merchant-level trends over time.
  • Net worth dashboard: A running snapshot of assets minus liabilities, including investment accounts and real estate if you connect or manually enter them.
  • Goals: Savings goals tied to specific accounts, with progress tracking and projected timelines.
  • Collaborative access: Multiple household members can share one account with individual logins — a feature that sets Monarch apart from most competitors.

Monarch also added an AI assistant in 2024 that lets you query your own financial data in plain language — asking things like "how much did I spend on restaurants in the last 90 days" and getting an actual answer rather than a redirect to a report.

Setup and Account Syncing

Connecting accounts is straightforward for major banks and credit unions. Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Fidelity, Schwab, and most large institutions link reliably and update daily. The friction shows up with smaller regional banks and credit unions, where Plaid coverage can be inconsistent — a limitation that is not unique to Monarch but is worth knowing upfront.

Manual account entry is available for institutions that cannot be linked, and Monarch does a decent job of letting you maintain those manually with periodic balance updates. Investment accounts pull in holdings and performance data, though the investment analytics are useful rather than deep — you are not getting the kind of portfolio analysis you would from a dedicated tool like Empower (formerly Personal Capital).

One genuine advantage: Monarch allows users to merge accounts across two people in one household without forcing everyone to share a single login. Each person gets their own credentials and can see a combined view. For couples managing money together, this is a significant quality-of-life feature that competitors either do not offer or charge extra for.

The Budgeting System — Flexible but Requires Commitment

Monarch uses a category-based budgeting system with a monthly structure. You can create custom categories and subcategories, set spending targets, and track progress throughout the month. The interface shows budgeted versus actual spending with clear visual indicators, and you can add rollover behavior to categories where unspent money carries forward — useful for irregular expenses like car maintenance or medical costs.

The system is less opinionated than YNAB, which is both a strength and a weakness. YNAB forces a specific methodology on you. Monarch lets you build the structure that fits your life, which means it works better once it is set up but requires more initial thinking to configure well. If you want the app to teach you a budgeting philosophy, look elsewhere. If you already have a system and want a tool that supports it without fighting you, Monarch is a strong fit.

Transaction categorization is automatic but imperfect — as it is everywhere. Monarch's rule engine for overriding and training categories is among the better implementations we have tested. You can set rules by merchant, keyword, or transaction amount range, and those rules apply retroactively if you choose. Over a few weeks of active use, the auto-categorization becomes noticeably more accurate.

The AI Features — Promising, Not Yet Essential

Monarch's AI assistant, which queries your personal transaction data, represents a genuine improvement over digging through filters manually. Asking natural-language questions and getting direct answers — with source transactions listed — is faster than building custom reports. The assistant handles most common queries well: spending by category, comparisons across time periods, identifying recurring charges.

Where it falls short is in more interpretive tasks. Ask it to analyze your spending habits and suggest adjustments, and the responses become generic in ways that feel like a chatbot rather than a financial advisor. The AI is a good retrieval tool today. It is not yet a reasoning tool. That limitation is fair given where the technology is across the industry, but it should calibrate your expectations before you subscribe expecting an AI that proactively manages your finances.

Monarch has indicated ongoing investment in these features, and the foundation — having your actual financial data in a structured, queryable form — is the right starting point for AI that eventually becomes more useful.

Pricing

At the time of writing, Monarch Money costs $14.99 per month or $99.99 per year (roughly $8.33/month billed annually). There is no free tier — only a seven-day free trial. The annual plan is the better value for anyone serious about using the product long-term.

That price point is higher than free tools that have historically dominated this space, and it is a legitimate consideration. The counterargument is that Monarch's business model depends on subscriptions rather than selling your data or pushing financial products — which is exactly why Mint was free and exactly why Mint no longer exists. You are paying for a product that has an incentive to serve you rather than monetize you. Whether that trade-off is worth $100 a year depends entirely on how actively you use it.

For couples using the shared household feature, the subscription covers both users — which makes the per-person cost more reasonable than it appears at first glance.

Real Weaknesses Worth Knowing

No review that glosses over the problems is worth reading. Monarch has real ones:

  • Syncing reliability: Some institutions break their Plaid connections periodically and require re-authentication. This is an industry-wide issue but it is annoying, and Monarch's in-app error messaging when a sync fails is not always helpful in diagnosing the problem.
  • No bill pay or direct financial actions: Monarch is a tracking and planning tool only. You cannot pay bills, transfer money, or take any action on your finances from within the app. It is read-only by design.
  • Investment depth is limited: Net worth tracking and basic portfolio visibility are solid. Deep investment analytics — asset allocation analysis, tax-loss harvesting suggestions, fee benchmarking — are not in scope here.
  • Learning curve for new budgeters: The flexibility that experienced budgeters love can feel overwhelming to someone setting up a budget for the first time with no existing framework.
  • No desktop app: The web interface is good, but there is no native desktop application for macOS or Windows if that matters to you.
What we liked
  • Clean, well-designed interface across web and mobile
  • Household sharing with separate logins — genuinely useful for couples
  • Highly customizable category rules that improve over time
  • Subscription model means no data selling or product upsells
  • Strong cash flow reporting and net worth tracking
  • AI assistant gives direct answers from your own transaction data
  • Annual plan brings cost down to around $8/month
What we didn't
  • No free tier — only a 7-day trial before committing
  • Account syncing occasionally breaks, especially with smaller institutions
  • Investment analytics are basic compared to dedicated tools
  • AI features are useful for retrieval but not yet for financial reasoning
  • Flexible budgeting system requires existing framework to get the most value
  • Read-only platform — no bill pay or financial actions
The Verdict

Monarch Money is the most complete personal finance app available for most households right now, and monarchmoney.com is where you should go if you are serious about having one place to track your full financial picture. It is not perfect — sync reliability is an ongoing industry problem, and the AI features need more development before they justify the marketing around them. But the budgeting system is genuinely flexible, the household sharing is rare and well-executed, and the subscription model aligns the company's interests with yours rather than against them. If you were a Mint user looking for a replacement, or if you have never found a finance app that actually fit the way you think about money, Monarch is the most honest answer on the market.
